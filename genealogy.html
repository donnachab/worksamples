Gathering What You Already Know

Your research begins not on the internet, but with the information and relatives you already have access to.

Prerequisites

  • A notebook, digital document, or audio recorder for interviews.
  • A clear system for labeling files and photos (e.g., "Smith_John_1880_BirthCert.jpg").

Steps

  1. Interview Living Relatives (The Irish Context): This is the most critical first step. When asking questions, be specific to the Irish experience:
    • "What county, or better, what parish, or better still, what townland were they from?" (This is vital, as "Ireland" is not a useful starting point).
    • "Do you know if the family was Catholic or Protestant?" (This is the single most important question, as it determines which set of records you will search).
    • "Did anyone ever mention an 'American Wake'?" (The term for the last party for an emigrant, which was a common cultural event).
    • "Do you have any letters from 'The Yank' or relatives who emigrated?"
  2. Locate "Home Records" (Irish Specifics): Search (with permission!) for physical documents. Look for items with a strong Irish context:
    • Memorial Cards / "In Memoriam" Cards: These are common in Irish Catholic culture and are genealogically rich.
    • The Family Bible: Note that this tradition was far more common in Protestant families.
    • Old letters, postcards, and any land documents or rent receipts.
  3. Organize and Chart the Data: Create a folder for each main surname and scan all physical documents. Once organized, you can effectively move to the digital phase.

Formulating a Research Question (Context)

A good next-step research question should be specific, guiding you directly to a record search, rather than being a broad, unanswerable query.

Vague Question
"Where in Ireland was Patrick Murphy born?" (Too broad; requires searching 32 counties)
Specific Question
"What was the name of Patrick Murphy's mother, as recorded on his marriage certificate in New York in 1888?" (Specific record, specific year, specific answer)

Result

You will have a foundational collection of names, dates, locations, and stories that will form the basis of your online research and prevent you from chasing incorrect leads.

Note: The Hierarchy of Irish Place Names: Always seek the Townland, as it is the most consistent geographical address for finding historical records. Relatives are more likely to remember the Parish (the church unit), but the Townland (the precise land unit) is the detail required for searching land records and pre-1864 church registers.

To help jog their memories, check these sites: Once you hear any place name, use these free tools to check its spelling and location, as records rely on the Townland:

  • Townlands.ie: A searchable database of all Irish townlands. (Search here)
  • Logainm.ie: The official Placenames Database of Ireland, excellent for finding variations and the original Irish spellings. (Search here)

Choosing Your Genealogy Software

A good genealogy program is essential for organizing your findings, visualizing relationships, and syncing with online record collections.

Prerequisites

  • You should have your initial family information collected, as described in Gathering What You Already Know.

Steps: Integrating Platform Choice

The choice of your main research platform should be based on where your ancestor settled after leaving Ireland:

  1. Choose Your Irish-based Hub (Findmypast):

    If your ancestor lived in Ireland or emigrated to the UK, you should prioritize Findmypast because it has the strongest collections of Irish parish records and the exclusive 1921 Census of England and Wales. Go to Findmypast.com.

  2. Choose Your Diaspora-based Hub (Ancestry):

    If your ancestor emigrated to the USA, Canada, or Australia, you should prioritize Ancestry because it has the largest global user tree database, resulting in more DNA matches and stronger collections of US/Canadian passenger lists and US Naturalization records. Go to Ancestry.com.

  3. Install a Desktop Program for Control:

    To maintain ownership and control over your data, install a local desktop program to sync with your online hub. This is your permanent backup for all records and citations:

    • For Mac/Windows, the most popular choice is Family Tree Maker (Download Here).
    • For a universal option that syncs with both Ancestry and FamilySearch, use RootsMagic (Download Here).
    • For the free, open-source solution offering maximum privacy, use Gramps (Download Here).
  4. Integrate Free Government Records:

    Always cross-reference with the essential free government and collaborative sources:

    • Civil Registration: Use IrishGenealogy.ie.
    • Catholic Registers: Use the browsable images at the National Library of Ireland (NLI).
    • Global Records: Use the free resources at FamilySearch.org.

Result

You have selected a primary tool and established a robust backup system, optimizing your process to find and secure records along your ancestor's migration path.

Understanding Key Record Types

Genealogy is built on records, which are grouped into several key categories.

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

Always try to find a Primary Source (a record created at the time of the event, like a birth certificate or a census page) rather than a Secondary Source (a record created later, like a family history book or an online tree). A primary source is more likely to be accurate.

Common Record Categories

Vital Records (or Civil Registration)
These are government-issued records of Births, Marriages, and Deaths (often called "BMDs"). In Ireland, these are a key topic in Using Civil Registration.
Census Records
A government-run headcount of the population, usually taken every 10 years. They are invaluable for placing a family in a specific location at a specific time and identifying all members of a household. See Using the 1901 & 1911 Censuses for the most important Irish examples.
Church Records (Parish Registers)
Before government registration, local churches were the primary record-keepers. These include records of baptisms, marriages, and burials, which often pre-date vital records by hundreds of years. See Finding Church Records.
Land & Property Records
In Ireland, records of land valuation and tax (like Griffith's Valuation) are a critical substitute for lost census records.
Immigration & Travel Records
Includes Passenger Lists (showing who was on a ship) and Naturalization Records (showing when an immigrant became a citizen of a new country).

Core Genealogy Terms

This reference list defines the common terms and acronyms you will encounter during your research.

Glossary

Ancestor
A person from whom you are directly descended (e.g., a parent, grandparent, great-grandparent).
BMD
An acronym for Births, Marriages, and Deaths. These are the three key "vital records."
Brick Wall
A research problem that seems impossible to solve, where you can no longer find information on a specific ancestor. See Breaking Down "Brick Walls" for techniques.
Citation (or Source)
A note detailing exactly where you found a piece of information (e.Example, "1901 Census of Ireland, Dublin, Mountjoy, DED 3/1, household 42"). Citing your sources is the most important habit in genealogy.
Civil Registration
The government system for officially recording births, marriages, and deaths. See Using Civil Registration.
Descendant
A person who is directly descended from a specific ancestor (e.g., you are a descendant of your great-grandparent).
Parish
A church administrative area. In Ireland, this is a critical geographical unit for finding church records. See Irish Geography.
Townland
The smallest and most important geographical division in Ireland. It is the "ancestral address" you will be searching for. See Irish Geography.

What Makes Irish Research Unique?

Irish research is famously challenging, primarily due to the catastrophic loss of records in the 1922 Four Courts fire.

The 1922 Four Courts Fire

During the Irish Civil War, a fire at the Public Record Office at the Four Courts in Dublin destroyed centuries of irreplaceable records. The most devastating losses for genealogists were:

  • Most Church of Ireland parish registers (pre-1870).
  • The vast majority of 19th-century census records (1821, 1831, 1841, 1851).
  • Over 1,000 years of wills, court records, and administrative documents.

Because of this, you cannot simply "go back" one census at a time as you can in other countries. You must use "census substitutes" and understand the two distinct periods of Irish research: pre- and post-Famine.

Understanding Irish Naming Patterns

Irish families, particularly before the 20th century, often followed a strict and traditional naming pattern for their children.

Traditional Naming Conventions

If you are missing a parent's name, you can often make an educated guess by applying these rules. This pattern was not universal, but it was extremely common.

  • The 1st son was named after the paternal grandfather (his father's father).
  • The 2nd son was named after the maternal grandfather (his mother's father).
  • The 3rd son was named after the father.
  • The 1st daughter was named after the maternal grandmother (her mother's mother).
  • The 2nd daughter was named after the paternal grandmother (her father's mother).
  • The 3rd daughter was named after the mother.

Later children were often named after the parents' siblings. Seeing this pattern in a family group is strong evidence you have the correct family, especially when dealing with common names like John Murphy or Mary O'Brien.

"Mac" vs. "O" and Latin Names

  • Mac & O: "Mac" means "son of" (e.g., MacCarthy) and "O" means "grandson of" (e.g., O'Sullivan). These prefixes were often dropped in the 17th-19th centuries due to English anglicization and then re-adopted later. Don't be surprised to see an "O'Brien" ancestor listed as "Brien" in an 1850s record.
  • Latin: Catholic parish registers were kept in Latin. This means you will see names like Patricius (Patrick), Ioannes (John), Maria (Mary), Jacobus (James), and Honora (Norah).

Irish Geography: Townlands & Parishes

You are not looking for an ancestor from "Ireland" or even "County Cork." You are looking for them in a Townland, the smallest and most critical unit of Irish geography.

The "Genealogical Address"

Almost all major Irish records (census, land, and church) are organized by geographical division. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for finding records.

  • Townland: The smallest, most ancient land unit. This is the "ancestral address." There are over 61,000 of them.
  • Civil Parish: A group of townlands. This was the main unit for tax and land records (like Griffith's Valuation).
  • Barony: A larger unit, now mostly obsolete, but used in some older records.
  • County: The main administrative unit everyone is familiar with (e.g., County Galway, County Antrim).

Separately, the churches had their own divisions. The Roman Catholic Parish often had different boundaries and names from the Civil Parish, which is a common point of confusion.

Key Irish Archives & Repositories

These are the primary online databases and physical archives that host the core Irish record collections.

Online Repositories (Free)

IrishGenealogy.ie
The official Irish government site. It provides free access to the indexes and images of Civil Registration (BMDs) and many historic Church Records.
The National Archives of Ireland
The most important free archive. It hosts the 1901 & 1911 Census, Tithe Applotment Books, historical wills, and much more.
National Library of Ireland (NLI)
Holds the free digital collection of Catholic Parish Register microfilms. The records are images only (not indexed by name), but they are essential for pre-1864 research.
FamilySearch.org
A massive, free database with many indexed Irish collections, including civil registration, land records, and probate records. Always check here.

Online Repositories (Subscription)

Findmypast.ie
A leading commercial site with extensive, exclusive Irish collections, including parish records, land records, and the largest collection of Irish newspapers. It is the partner of the National Archives of Ireland.
Ancestry.com
The world's largest commercial genealogy site. While its Irish-specific collection is smaller than Findmypast's, it has excellent US and UK records for tracking emigrant ancestors.
RootsIreland.ie
A subscription site run by the Irish Family History Foundation. It features transcriptions of parish records from local genealogy centers across Ireland. It can be particularly good for finding records that aren't online elsewhere.

Physical Archives (In-Person Research)

The National Archives (Dublin)
The primary physical repository for state records.
The National Library (Dublin)
Holds estate papers, newspapers, and the original Catholic parish register microfilms.
The Valuation Office (Dublin)
Holds the "cancelled" land books that track Griffith's Valuation forward in time, showing changes in property ownership.
PRONI (Belfast)
The Public Record Office of Northern Ireland. This is the main archive for records relating to the six counties of Northern Ireland.

Using Land Records (Griffith's & Tithes)

These two land valuation records are the most important census substitutes for finding your family in mid-19th century Ireland.

Prerequisites

  • You must know the name of an ancestor living in Ireland between the 1820s and 1850s.
  • You must know the County and ideally the Parish they lived in. You can find this using the tools in Irish Geography.

Steps

  1. Start with Griffith's Valuation (1847-1864): This was a comprehensive valuation of all land and property in Ireland. Every head of household leasing or owning property is listed.
    • Go to the Ask About Ireland website, which provides the complete resource for free.
    • Search by your ancestor's name and the county.
    • The results will show you the ancestor (Occupier), the person they leased from (Immediate Lessor), the Civil Parish, and the Townland.
    • Crucially, note the map reference number (e.g., "5a"). You can use the accompanying maps to see the exact location of their house or plot.
  2. Move to the Tithe Applotment Books (1823-1837): This is the " prequel" to Griffith's. It was a survey to determine the tithes (a tax) payable to the Church of Ireland.
    • Note: It generally only lists heads of households who were farmers or held land (not city residents or landless laborers) and is less comprehensive than Griffith's.
    • Search the free collection at The National Archives of Ireland.
    • Cross-reference what you find with the Griffith's data. Finding a family in the same townland in both records is a very strong sign of continuity.

Result

You will have pinpointed your ancestor to a specific townland and parish, which is the "ancestral address" you need to find the next set of records: church registers.

Finding Church Records

Church registers of baptism, marriage, and burial are the primary source for pre-famine births, marriages, and deaths.

Prerequisites

  • You must have a good idea of your ancestor's religion (e.g., Catholic, Church of Ireland, Presbyterian).
  • You must know the Parish and County where they lived, ideally found using Land Records.

Steps

  1. Determine the Correct Parish: Remember from Irish Geography that the Civil Parish (used for land records) and the Catholic Parish (used for church records) may have different names or boundaries. Use John Grenham's site to check.
  2. Search Based on Denomination: Proceed with the appropriate search path based on the religious affiliation of your ancestor:
    • If Catholic:
      1. Start Free (Browsing): Go to the National Library of Ireland (NLI). This site holds the free digital images of almost all Catholic parish registers up to 1880. Note that these are not indexed by name; you must browse them page by page.
      2. Search by Name (Free Indexing): Check IrishGenealogy.ie and FamilySearch.org. Both have free, searchable indexes (and often images) for many Catholic parishes.
      3. Search by Name (Subscription Indexing): Check Findmypast and RootsIreland.ie. RootsIreland is particularly strong due to transcriptions from local heritage centers.
    • If Protestant (Church of Ireland / Presbyterian):
      1. Search Church of Ireland (Anglican) Registers: Note that most pre-1922 registers were lost.
        • Free Access: Check IrishGenealogy.ie, which is the official home for many surviving Church of Ireland registers (e.g., for Dublin, Kerry, Carlow).
        • Transcripts & Lists: Visit the RCB Library in Dublin (or its website) for the updated list of parish registers.
      2. Search Presbyterian Records (Mainly Ulster): These records were generally not lost in the 1922 fire.
        • Public Record Office of Northern Ireland (PRONI): PRONI holds microfilm copies of nearly all Presbyterian registers for Ulster. Search their online catalogue, or view digitized records on-site.
        • Presbyterian Historical Society (PHSI): The PHSI holds microfilms of records similar to PRONI and also holds unique, original documents.
        • Subscription Access: Transcriptions of many Presbyterian registers are available on RootsIreland.ie.

Result

With luck and persistence, you will find a baptismal or marriage record for your ancestor, which may be the only "birth" or "marriage" record that ever existed for them.

Guide to the 1821-1851 Censuses

This reference guide explains the status of the 19th-century Irish census returns, which were almost entirely destroyed in the 1922 Four Courts fire.

What Was Lost?

The complete, all-island census returns for 1821, 1831, 1841, and 1851 were lost in the fire. This is the "great brick wall" of Irish genealogy. The 1861, 1871, 1881, and 1891 returns were pulped for paper by the government before the fire (a separate, tragic loss).

What Survives?

Only tiny, scattered fragments and transcripts survive. You should always perform a quick search, but be aware that the chances of finding an ancestor are very low. All surviving fragments are free to search on the National Archives of Ireland website.

1821 Census
Fragments survive for parts of Cavan, Fermanagh, Galway, Meath, and Offaly (King's).
1831 Census
Fragments survive for parts of Derry/Londonderry.
1841 Census
Only one complete parish (Killeshandra, Co. Cavan) survives. All other fragments for all of Ireland were destroyed.
1851 Census
Fragments survive for parts of Antrim, Belfast, and Dublin.

Using Civil Registration (BMDs)

This task explains how to find the government-issued records of Births, Marriages, and Deaths (BMDs).

Prerequisites

  • You must be looking for an event that happened after the start dates.
  • Key Dates:
    • 1845: Start of all non-Catholic marriages registration.
    • 1864: Start of all births, deaths, and Catholic marriages registration.
  • You need a name, a rough date, and a general location.

Steps

  1. Go to IrishGenealogy.ie: The official Irish government site, IrishGenealogy.ie, provides free access to the full indexes and record images. This should be your first and primary stop.
  2. Understand the Registration District: Records are not filed by county but by "Registration District," which was based on the old Poor Law Unions. Don't be surprised if an ancestor from "East Galway" is in the "Ballinasloe" or "Mountbellew" district.
  3. Search the Records:
    • Births (1864-1921): Provide the child's name, parents' names, and the Townland of birth. A birth certificate is the only record that gives the mother's maiden name.
    • Marriages (1845/1864-1921): Provide the bride and groom's names, ages, occupations, and (crucially) their fathers' names and occupations.
    • Deaths (1864-1921): Provide the deceased's name, age, cause of death, and the name of the "informant" (usually a relative who was present).
  4. Analyze the Image: Once you find an index entry, always click to view the actual register page. The image contains far more detail (like witnesses to a marriage or the exact townland of birth) than the index alone.

Result

You will have the official government record of your ancestor's life event, which provides the critical data (like parents' names or a specific address) needed to jump back to the next generation.

Using the 1901 & 1911 Censuses

These two census returns are the only complete, all-island censuses that are available to the public.

Prerequisites

  • You must have an ancestor who was living in Ireland in either 1901 or 1911.

Steps

  1. Go to the National Archives: The complete census records are free to search at the National Archives of Ireland website.
  2. Search for Your Ancestor: You can search by name, county, and census year. Be creative with spellings (e.g., "Murphy" might be "Murphey").
  3. Analyze the "Form A" (Household Return): This is the main page, filled out and signed by the "Head of Family." It lists:
    • Every person in the house on census night.
    • Their relationship to the head of family.
    • Their religion.
    • Their occupation.
    • Their age.
    • Crucially: "Read or Write?" (Yes/No) and "Where Born" (usually the county, or "England," "USA," etc.).
  4. Analyze the "Form B1" (House & Building Return): Don't skip this. Click the "Show all information" button. This form tells you what kind of house they lived in (e.g., "3 rooms, 3 windows in front"), what the walls were made of ("Stone, brick, or concrete"), and the roof ("Thatch" or "Slate"). It paints a vivid picture of their economic status.
  5. Note (1911 Census Only): The 1911 census also asked married women "How many years married," "Total children born alive," and "Children still living." This is genealogically priceless.

Result

You will have a complete snapshot of your entire family unit, frozen in time, providing a wealth of personal detail and confirming relationships, ages, and birth locations.

Wills and Probate Records

This reference details how to find wills, which can provide a huge amount of family information, list property, and name relatives.

What Exists?

Like other records, many original wills were lost in the 1922 Four Courts fire. However, the record books (copies) often survive. The system was centralized in 1858, making post-1858 wills much easier to find.

Wills (Post-1858)

These are the easiest to find. The "Calendars of Wills and Administrations" (which are indexes) survive intact from 1858 to the present.

  • How to Search: Go to the National Archives Wills Calendars. This free database is a complete index for all of Ireland from 1858-1922.
  • The index gives you the name, date of death, place of death, and the "executors" of the will (often a son or wife). You can use this to order a copy of the full will.
Wills (Pre-1858)

This is much more difficult, as most original wills were destroyed. You are looking for copies or indexes that survived.

  • FamilySearch has many digitized records, including indexes to "Prerogative" wills (for wealthier individuals).
  • The National Archives site has some surviving "Will Books" from local district registries.

Waves of Irish Emigration

Irish emigration was a centuries-long, often traumatic phenomenon that fundamentally shaped the nation. Knowing the timeline is key to knowing which records to search.

Major Waves and Destinations

Pre-Famine (1700s to 1845)

Emigration was primarily driven by economic opportunity and, for Ulster Scots (Presbyterians) and others, religious freedom.

  • Destinations: The American colonies and Canada (especially the Maritimes).
  • The 'Forgotten' Migration: Large numbers of Presbyterians left Ulster for the American Atlantic shores from the 1680s onward, often referred to as "Scotch-Irish" in the US.
The Great Famine (1845-1852)

Driven by mass starvation, this wave saw nearly 2 million people flee the island, many of them Catholic and from poorer, Irish-speaking areas.

  • Destinations: North America (Canada and the US), and Great Britain.
  • Record Note: The four years immediately following the famine saw even more emigration, as conditions convinced more people to leave.
Post-Famine / Economic Migration (Late 19th Century)

Emigration became a planned, self-perpetuating system, often funded by earlier emigrants sending back "remittance" money to pay for a relative's ticket.

  • Destinations: The US remained the primary destination, but major waves began targeting Australia and New Zealand.
20th Century & Modern Waves

Mass emigration accelerated again in the 1950s (primarily to Great Britain) and in the 1980s (to the US and UK) due to poor economic conditions in independent Ireland.

Finding Passenger Lists

Passenger lists are essential, as they often record the ancestor's name, age, occupation, and, critically, their last place of residence (sometimes listing the county in Ireland).

Prerequisites

  • The immigrant's name and approximate year of arrival in the destination country (e.g., USA, Canada, Australia).
  • A rough idea of the Port of Arrival (e.g., New York, Boston, Quebec).

Key US Port Search Tools (Context)

The US is broken into three main eras based on its New York processing facilities:

Castle Garden Era (1855-1890, covering lists from 1820-1891)
The records for this era are available digitally through several sources, including the free Ellis Island/Castle Garden Passenger Search. You can also access the Famine Irish Passenger Record File (1846-1851) at the National Archives (NARA) Access to Archival Databases website.
Ellis Island Era (1892-1954)
The vast majority of late-19th and early-20th century Irish immigrants passed through Ellis Island in New York. The records are available for free search on the Statue of Liberty-Ellis Island Foundation's Passenger Database.
  1. Identify the correct arrival port and era based on your ancestor's known arrival year.
  2. Search the relevant port database (Castle Garden/Ellis Island for New York, or commercial sites for other ports).
  3. Apply Searching Tips:
    • Spelling: Names were recorded at the Port of Departure (in Ireland/UK), not the US, and were often spelled phonetically. Be sure to try alternative spellings.
    • Boston/Philadelphia: Many ships landed in ports other than New York, especially during the Famine years. Search the collections on commercial sites like Ancestry.com or Findmypast.com, which cover Boston, Philadelphia, and Baltimore records.
    • Canada/UK Departures: Before the US cracked down on immigration, many Irish emigrants first went to Canada or the UK and then traveled overland or on shorter cross-channel routes to the US. They will appear on Canadian or UK outbound manifests.

Result

You will have located the passenger list, which often records the ancestor's name, age, occupation, and last place of residence (sometimes listing the county in Ireland).

Irish Genealogy Guide > 5. The Emigration Story > Using Naturalization Records

Using Naturalization Records

A naturalization record, or "Second Papers," is often the single greatest genealogical discovery for an immigrant ancestor. It contains details often found nowhere else.

Prerequisites

You need to know the name, approximate date of arrival, and country of settlement for an immigrant ancestor.

Naturalization Record Value

A formal naturalization petition, particularly those filed after 1906, can list:

  • Exact Date of Birth and Place of Birth (often down to the townland or parish in Ireland).
  • Exact Date and Port of Arrival.
  • Spouse and Children's Names, Dates of Birth, and sometimes their Places of Residence.

Steps

  1. Understand the Two-Step Process: Naturalization was generally a two-step process that took at least five years:
    1. Declaration of Intention ("First Papers"): Filed after two years of residency. This is a shorter document that declares the intent to become a citizen.
    2. Petition for Naturalization ("Second Papers"): Filed after three additional years. This is the document that contains the detailed genealogical information.
  2. Search Records in the USA (Pre-1906): Before 1906, any court (local, county, state, or federal) could grant citizenship, meaning records are scattered. Search by location:
    • Check the National Archives (NARA) regional facility covering that area.
    • Commercial sites like Ancestry.com and Fold3 often have online indexes.
  3. Search Records in the USA (Post-1906): After 1906, records were centralized:
    • Request copies of federal court petitions (1906-1956) from the US Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) Genealogy Program.
  4. Search Records in Canada: Naturalization records are held by the Library and Archives Canada (LAC website). Search by the place and date the certificate was issued.

Result

You will have an official document that is often the single greatest genealogical discovery for an immigrant ancestor, containing details often found nowhere else.

Using US Census Records

The US Census is arguably the single most important tool for tracing an immigrant family, providing a ten-year snapshot that confirms location and often reveals Irish origins.

Prerequisites

  • You must know the familyâ€™s city or county of residence in the US between 1850 and 1940.
  • Remember that the US census is released 72 years after it is taken (the latest available census is 1950).

Key Census Questions (Context)

Focus on the details that bridge the gap back to Ireland, as these questions changed by census year. You can search the census for free on sites like FamilySearch and the National Archives (NARA), or commercially on Ancestry.com.

1850 - 1870 Censuses
These are crucial. They ask for the individual's Place of Birth (often simply listed as "Ireland"). They also list every person in the household, including domestic servants (often single Irish women).
1880 Census
This is one of the best. It asks for the birthplace of the individual, AND the birthplace of their Father, AND the birthplace of their Mother. This is invaluable for confirming Irish parentage.
1900, 1910, and 1920 Censuses
These ask for the year of Immigration and whether the person is a Naturalized Citizen or has filed their papers. If you find "Na" (Naturalized), immediately consult Naturalization Records.
1930 Census
Asks for the age at first marriage.

Steps

  1. Identify the relevant census year(s) based on the information you need (e.g., use the 1880 census to find parental birthplaces).
  2. Search the index on a commercial site (Ancestry/Findmypast) or a free archive (FamilySearch/NARA).
  3. Examine the original image and extract all relevant immigration, parentage, and naturalization details to build your case back to Ireland.

Result

You can track a family's movement over decades, verifying their life events and getting closer to their exact date and county of emigration from Ireland.

Finding US Vital Records

US Birth, Marriage, and Death (BMD) records can often provide the exact town or county of origin in Irelandâ€”far more specific than a census record's "Ireland."

Prerequisites

You need a name, date, and state/county where the vital event occurred.

Steps

  1. Prioritize the Death Certificate: The **death certificate** is often the most valuable record for an Irish immigrant, as it required the **exact birthplace** and **parentsâ€™ names** of the deceased.
  2. Identify the County/City: Use the census (see Using US Census Records) to pinpoint where the ancestor lived when the event occurred.
  3. Consult the State Archives: Every state manages its vital records differently. Search state archives or libraries for digitized indexes and images. For example, some states release death certificates over 50 years old to the public.
  4. Use Commercial Indexes: Commercial sites like Ancestry.com often have large collections of State and County indexes. For instance, they hold the indexes for the New York City BMDs, which are essential for many Irish families.
  5. Order the Original: Once you have a date and location from an index, you must contact the relevant State or County government's Vital Records Office to order the original certificate for the most reliable information.

Navigating English & Welsh Records

Since 1837, England and Wales have maintained a unified system of General Register Office (GRO) records, which are far more consistent than those in the US.

Prerequisites

You need an approximate date, name, and location in England or Wales after 1837.

Civil Registration Records (BMDs, Post-1837)

Understand the specific details provided by each record type:

Births
Certificates are indexed quarterly. They provide the date, place, father's name and occupation, and mother's maiden name (essential for the correct family tree).
Marriages
Indexed quarterly. They list the father's name and occupation for both the bride and groom, which helps confirm the family identity.
Deaths
The death certificate provides the deceased's name, age, occupation, and the name and address of the informant (usually a close relative).

Steps

  1. Search Indexes (Free): Use the FreeBMD (FreeBMD website) project for free access to the General Register Office (GRO) indexes, which cover 1837 to the 1980s.
  2. Order the Original (Official): Once you have a volume and page number, you can order an official certificate directly from the UK General Register Office (GRO).
  3. Search Parish Records (Pre-1837): For records before 1837, you must rely on Anglican (Church of England) parish registers. Search commercial sites like Findmypast, which has worked with county archives to digitize many of these collections.

Result

You will have official, detailed BMD records for your Irish ancestors who settled in England or Wales.

Navigating Scottish Records

Scotland has a distinct and much older system of centralized records, making genealogy research there often easier than in England or Ireland.

Prerequisites

You need an approximate date and location in Scotland.

Statutory Registration Details (Context)

Scottish records are renowned for their detail and completeness, starting in 1855:

  • The **1855-Only Records** include the parents' date and place of marriage on the birth record, and the parents' names on the death record.
  • **Deaths** list the deceased's parents' names (including the mother's maiden name), a key difference from English death records.

Steps

  1. Use the Official Site: Virtually all Scottish records are housed in one place. You must use the official, pay-per-view site, ScotlandsPeople.
  2. Search the Single Repository: This single site contains all major record sets, allowing you to cross-reference easily:
    • Old Parish Registers (church records, pre-1855).
    • Statutory Registers (BMDs, 1855 onwards).
    • Census Records (1841-1911).
    • Valuation Rolls (property/tax records).
    • Wills and Testaments.

Result

If your Irish ancestor settled in Scotland, this centralized approach often makes the records easier to find and provides much richer detail than other destination countries.

Breaking Down "Brick Walls"

A "brick wall" is the point where traditional paper records have been exhausted. With Irish ancestors, this usually happens in the pre-1864 period where records are scarce.

Prerequisites

You should have exhausted all primary paper records for your direct line ancestor.

Steps

  1. Focus on Siblings and Nieces/Nephews: Find all of your ancestor's siblings and trace their lives forward. They might have a census, military, or naturalization record that lists the specific Irish townland or parish where the main ancestor was born.
  2. Apply the Naming Pattern (in Reverse): Use the naming conventions detailed in Understanding Irish Naming Patterns to predict the grandparents' names. This helps eliminate potential families in the parish registers.
  3. Check Collateral Records: Search burial records, church registers, and cemetery plot records. Cemetery records often list everyone buried in a plot, which can connect otherwise unknown family members (aunts, cousins) to your direct line.
  4. Use Nicknames and Variants: Always search using phonetic spellings and nicknames. An ancestor recorded as "Kate" in the census may be "Catherine" or "Katherine" in the church register. Use wildcard characters for alternate letters and limit your search by setting a timeframe of events.
  5. Move to DNA: When all paper trails are exhausted, genetic genealogy is the next step (see Using DNA Testing for Genealogy).

Result

By shifting focus from the direct line to the wider family, you increase your chance of finding a collateral record that holds the key to your Irish origins.

Understanding Your DNA Results

Interpreting DNA results requires understanding the difference between Ethnicity Estimates and DNA Matching.

Ethnicity Estimates

This tells you where your DNA most closely resembles reference populations (e.g., "95% Irish").

  • Nuance: Ethnicity estimates are guides, not facts. They often show Ancestral Regions, which are clusters of matches likely related to a specific county or area of Ireland, making them useful for pinpointing a location.
  • Comparison: DNA testing websites present ethnicity in varying ways. You may see "Irish" on one site and "Scottish" or "British" on another due to the long history of migration between these islands.

DNA Matching and Clustering

DNA Matching is the gold standard for genealogy. It connects you to living cousins who share DNA with you.

  • Clustering: This technique involves organizing your DNA matches into distinct groups (or "clusters"). Everyone in a cluster is likely descended from a single common ancestral couple. This allows you to tackle your family tree by groups instead of individual names.
  • Actionable Matches: The most valuable matches are those who have a detailed public family tree attached to their results. You can use their tree to find the ancestral couple they share with you, which is the key to solving your brick wall.

Using Military Records

Irish ancestors served in numerous conflicts worldwide. Military records are often the only source containing specific physical descriptions, next-of-kin names, and the parish of birth.

Prerequisites

You need the ancestor's name, approximate birth year, and the conflict/force they served in (e.g., British Army, US Civil War).

Steps

  1. Search British Army Records (Pre-1922):
    • Record Details: Documents like Attestation Papers (enlistment forms) and Pension Claims often list the soldier's County and Parish of Birth, height, physical description, and the names of his wife and children.
    • Where to Search: The bulk of these records are held at The National Archives (UK) in Kew and are digitized on commercial sites like Findmypast and Ancestry.
    • Soldiers' Wills: The National Archives of Ireland holds a free, searchable index of the wills of Irish soldiers who died while serving in the British Army (mostly WWI).
  2. Search Irish Military Archives (Post-1913): Records relating to the revolutionary period and the Defence Forces are held in Ireland.
    • Revolutionary Records (1913-1923): Search the Military Service Pension Collection and the Bureau of Military History (1913-1921) online at the Irish Military Archives website.
    • Check the 1922 Army Census for members of the National Army.
  3. Search US Civil War Records (1861-1865):
    • Compiled Military Service Records (CMSR): These records often contain enlistment details, physical descriptions, and place of birth (county in Ireland). Search the National Archives (NARA) index and the free Civil War Soldiers and Sailors System database.

Result

You will have accessed detailed personal and origin information that often survives when other records have been lost.
